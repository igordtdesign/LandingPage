Overview

The paradox of choice across 220,000 games

Since 2003, Steam evolved from a distribution channel into the world's largest game marketplace, now hosting over 220,000 products (SteamDB, 2025). This expansion brought a classic UX problem: the paradox of choice.

Abundance doesn't lead to satisfaction - it leads to indecision and frustration. The current recommendation system prioritizes popularity and recent history, ignoring intent and context. The result: users can't describe what they want in natural language, and the system can't listen.

Central question: How can conversational artificial intelligence enhance the game discovery experience in digital marketplaces like Steam?

Identified Failures

What the heuristic analysis revealed

Around 30 anonymous browsing sessions (no login, no history) were conducted between July and August 2025 to avoid algorithmic bias. The failures concentrate around three of Nielsen's heuristics (1994).

↗ High frequency
Error prevention
The "realistic" tag indicates graphics, not gameplay mechanics. A user looking for realistic fishing simulation finds completely unrelated games. The system leads users astray without any warning.
↗ High frequency
Consistency and standards
The "immersive simulator" filter returns titles unrelated to the immersive sim genre. The "similar games" section for Deus Ex lists Spider-Man and Avatar - sharing only visual aspects, not structural ones.
→ Medium frequency
Flexibility and efficiency
Only a fraction of tags appear on the main screen. To find the "pirates" tag, a user had to access the Sea of Thieves page and discover it indirectly. The Curse of Monkey Island doesn't even have this tag.

Comparative analysis finding: The Epic Games Store, despite having fewer tags, offers an even more difficult journey - no category search field, and a genre menu buried in a long vertical list. Steam, despite its flaws, still delivers a superior UX compared to its main competitor.

Research Method

Exploratory qualitative research with personas

The research combined direct empirical observation (anonymous Steam browsing), heuristic analysis based on Nielsen (1994), and persona construction grounded in Cooper (2007) and Revella (2015).

Three personas were developed from real behaviors observed during the sessions, each exposing a distinct failure pattern in the recommendation system.

Methodological Path
  • Empirical observation — anonymous browsing, ~30 sessions (Jul–Aug 2025)
  • Systematic recording — screenshots and descriptive notes per journey
  • Heuristic analysis — mapping by violated Nielsen heuristic
  • Persona construction — integration of observation and literature
  • Comparative analysis — Steam vs. Epic Games Store vs. Xbox Game Pass
  • Critical synthesis — foundation for the SteamAI proposal

The Proposal — SteamAI

A chatbot as the intermediary layer between algorithm and user

SteamAI is conceived as a chatbot integrated into the Steam interface, acting as a mediator between the traditional recommendation system and the user - translating subjective preferences into humanized search parameters.

Interaction Flow
01
Natural language input
"I want a light-looking adventure game I can play for 30 minutes a day" or "recommend something like Disco Elysium, but shorter and focused on moral choices."
02
Contextual AI processing
Semantic analysis of the request cross-referenced with the game database - descriptions, tags, reviews, and history - recognizing preferences, mood, and time constraints.
03
Recommendations with reasoning
Each result comes with an explanation: "I recommended this game because you showed interest in short narratives and moral choices." - the core principle of XAI (Explainable AI).
04
Iterative refinement
The user can adjust the request, dismiss suggestions with one click, or deepen the dialogue. With each interaction, the system learns and refines the player's profile.
3 UX Pillars
Clarity and empathy
The system explains how recommendations are generated. The user understands the "why" behind each suggestion - reducing the sense of arbitrariness that dominates the current model.
Flexibility and control
Unlike automatic lists, SteamAI offers active interactivity. The dialogue creates a refinement cycle that makes the experience participatory.
Responsible personalization
The system operates within ethical and transparent limits, in compliance with the LGPD (Brazilian Data Protection Law). It doesn't manipulate decisions - it expands the capacity to choose.

Design Decisions

Why each decision was made

Explainable AI (XAI) as a functional principle
Good recommendations aren't enough - the user needs to understand why. Each suggestion comes with a natural language justification. This isn't polish: it's what transforms an arbitrary recommendation into a trust relationship. Grounded in the EU Ethics Guidelines (2019) and Brazil's LGPD.
Native integration with the Steam interface
SteamAI doesn't replace the existing system - it complements it. The chatbot appears as a floating icon in the store, opening a familiar pop-up that matches the platform's dark visual language. Steam's UI is deeply embedded in user expectations.
Emotional design (Norman, 2004)
The system operates at three levels: visceral (the comfortable chat aesthetic), behavioral (the fluency of dialogue), and reflective (the sense of being understood). The user doesn't just search for a game - they feel heard.
Iterative refinement as the core loop
With each interaction, the system learns and adjusts. The user can dismiss suggestions, redirect the search, or deepen the dialogue. Discovery becomes a collaborative process between human and machine - not a query to an oracle.
Expected Benefits

What SteamAI changes — for the user and for the platform

For the user, the experience becomes humanized and comprehensible, reducing cognitive effort and time spent searching. The sense of control and genuine personalization increase satisfaction and trust.

For Valve, the gains come on two fronts: increased conversion rates - users who find what they want buy more - and prolonged retention, as the discovery journey becomes a positive part of the consumption experience.

Replicability: SteamAI represents a conceptual model applicable to other contexts - streaming, e-commerce, educational platforms - where personalized and ethical curation are competitive differentiators.

Acknowledged Limitations
  • Technical: incomplete and inconsistent metadata in Steam's catalog would compromise initial recommendation accuracy
  • Ethical: use of behavioral data requires explicit LGPD compliance and transparency about what is collected
  • Operational: continuous AI model maintenance requires specialized teams and constant human curation
  • Methodological: no interviews with real users — personas were built through observation and literature, without in-person validation
  • Tone of voice: an overly technical chatbot alienates casual users; too generic creates frustration — the balance needs empirical testing
Study Conclusions

What the study demonstrated

Discovery is an ethical challenge, not just a technical one
Improving Steam's recommendations isn't a matter of more sophisticated algorithms. It's an ethical and emotional design challenge - where understanding the user is as important as processing their data.
Transparency is a feature, not cosmetic
XAI (Explainable AI) isn't a detail - it's what transforms automated recommendation into meaningful interaction. Without explaining the why, the system remains a black box and generates distrust.
Conversational design is UX design
The tone of voice, message length, and chatbot timing require the same care as any visual component. Digital empathy isn't an abstraction - it's a design decision in every interaction.

The advancement of artificial intelligence in digital environments must be guided by three interdependent dimensions: technical efficiency, explanatory clarity, and human meaning. SteamAI represents not just a conceptual solution, but a proposal for dialogue between data and people.

— Igor Dalmolin Tronco, PUCRS Postgraduate Thesis 2025
